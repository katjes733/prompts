I want help making informed voting decisions for an upcoming election. Please guide me 
through this step by step, one question at a time. Ask a single question as plain 
text, wait for my typed answer, then ask the next. Never present multiple questions 
in the same message. Do not move to the next step until I have answered the current 
question.

Do not use interactive forms, buttons, checkboxes, or widgets for collecting my 
answers. All questions should be asked as plain conversational text. For each 
question, list the available options clearly as a numbered or lettered list so I 
can reply with a number, letter, or short phrase. Always include an "Other" or 
"Not sure" option where appropriate.

After receiving each answer, begin your next message by briefly acknowledging 
what you heard, e.g. "Got it — you selected Economy, Education, and Climate." 
Then ask the next question. This confirms the answer was registered before moving on.

Use the visualizer / artifact panel exclusively for presenting research findings, 
ballot summaries, candidate profiles, and the final voter guide. All visual output 
— ballot overviews, candidate cards, fit assessments, recommendation summaries — 
should be rendered as rich visual artifacts. Never render findings as plain text 
in the chat when a visual artifact would be clearer.

## Step 1 — Identify the election

Ask me the following questions one at a time:

1. What country or region are you voting in?
   List options:
   a) United States
   b) Canada
   c) United Kingdom
   d) Germany
   e) Australia
   f) Other — please specify

2. What state, province, or region? 
   For the United States, ask me to type my state name or abbreviation.
   For other countries, ask for the equivalent subdivision.

3. What is the specific city, county, or locality you are voting in?
   Ask me to type it, with the example: "e.g. Gilbert, Maricopa County"

4. What is the election date or name?
   Ask me to type it, with examples: 
   "e.g. July 21, 2026 · November 2026 general · Super Tuesday 2026"

Once I have answered these four questions, independently research the election 
using web search to determine:
- The official election type (partisan primary, nonpartisan primary, general 
  election, ranked choice, jungle primary, ballot measures only, or mixed)
- Which offices and races are on the ballot for my specific jurisdiction
- Any relevant election rules (e.g. does my state have open or closed primaries? 
  Can independents request a party ballot? Are there voter registration deadlines 
  I should know about?)

Present your findings as a rich visual artifact — a structured ballot overview 
showing every race found, the election type, and any important rules. Then ask 
me in plain text:

"Does this look correct and complete? Type Yes to continue, or describe anything 
that is missing or wrong."

If I flag something, research it, update the visual artifact, and ask again 
before moving on.

## Step 2 — Research the ballot

Use web search to find every race on my ballot based on my location and the 
election identified in Step 1. This includes:
- Federal races (President, U.S. Senate, U.S. House)
- Statewide races (Governor, Attorney General, Secretary of State, and other 
  executive offices)
- State legislative races (Senate and House for my specific district)
- County races (sheriff, assessor, recorder, clerk of court, supervisor, etc.)
- Local/municipal races (mayor, city or town council, school board, fire 
  district, etc.)
- Ballot measures, propositions, amendments, or referenda

Do not assume any race is uncontested without verifying via web search. Candidate 
lists change — always confirm with current sources.

Present the full ballot as a rich visual artifact — a structured list of every 
race with candidates listed under each. Then ask in plain text:

"Does this ballot look complete? Type Yes to continue, or tell me what is 
missing or incorrect."

If I flag something, research it, update the visual artifact, and ask again 
before moving on.

## Step 3 — Collect my priorities

Ask each section below as a plain text question, one at a time. Wait for my 
typed answer and acknowledge it before asking the next.

**A. Location**
"What is your specific city, neighborhood, or zip code? This helps me confirm 
your exact district assignments. e.g. Gilbert AZ or 85296"

**B. Party / ballot selection**
Only ask this if the election type requires it (partisan primary).
If nonpartisan or general election: skip to section C.

"Which party's primary ballot will you be voting on? Type the number of your choice:
1) Democratic Party
2) Republican Party
3) Libertarian Party
4) Green Party
5) Other party — please specify
6) I am registered independent or not sure — help me figure out which ballot 
   best matches my values"

If I select option 6: collect sections C and D first, then analyze my issue 
positions and recommend which party's primary ballot best matches my stated 
priorities before proceeding to candidate research. Present the analysis as a 
visual artifact, then ask me to confirm my choice in plain text.

**C. Issues that matter most to me**
"Which issues matter most to you? Type the numbers of all that apply — 
you can select as many as you like:

 1) Economy / cost of living (jobs, wages, inflation, household budgets)
 2) Education / public schools (funding, curriculum, teacher pay, school choice)
 3) Healthcare / medical costs (access, affordability, insurance, drug prices)
 4) Immigration / border policy (enforcement, pathways, asylum, workforce)
 5) Climate / environment / water (clean energy, emissions, water security, drought)
 6) Housing affordability (supply, zoning, rent, homeownership)
 7) Democracy / election integrity (voting access, election administration, rule of law)
 8) Public safety / crime (policing, criminal justice, community safety)
 9) Bipartisanship / cross-aisle cooperation (ability to work with the other side)
10) Government transparency / accountability (open records, oversight, ethics)
11) Infrastructure (roads, bridges, utilities, water systems, broadband)
12) Technology / AI policy (innovation, regulation, cybersecurity, digital rights)
13) Fiscal responsibility / balanced budgets (spending discipline, debt, taxes)
14) Reproductive rights / healthcare freedom
15) Veterans / military families
16) Small business / entrepreneurship
17) Gun policy (rights, safety measures, regulations)
18) Foreign policy / national security
19) Social equity / civil rights
20) Local quality of life (parks, services, community character, growth management)"

**D. Candidate style preference**
"What kind of candidate are you looking for? Type the number of your choice:

1) Pragmatic centrist — electable, works across the aisle, favors incremental 
   progress
2) Progressive — bold policy vision, energizes the base, structural change
3) Experienced insider — institutional knowledge, proven track record of delivery
4) Outsider / reformer — challenges the status quo, grassroots, disrupts 
   entrenched power
5) Community-focused local leader — neighborhood roots, pragmatic on local issues
6) No strong preference — match me to whoever fits my issue positions best"

**E. Local controversies**
Before asking this section, use web search to research the top local issues and 
controversies relevant to my specific ballot. Then ask about the top 2–3 local 
flashpoint issues one at a time, each as a numbered list with a neutral framing 
and 3–4 position options plus "No opinion / not familiar with this issue."
Do not ask more than 3 local controversy questions total.

**F. Dealbreakers and open notes**
"Is there anything else you want me to factor in? This could be a dealbreaker, 
a strongly held value, a candidate you already know you like or dislike, or any 
other context. Type your answer or type Skip to continue."

**Confirmation step**
After collecting all answers in sections A through F, present a visual artifact 
summarizing everything I said — my location, party selection, top issues, 
candidate style preference, local controversy positions, and any open notes. 
Then ask in plain text:

"Does this summary look accurate? Type Yes to start the research, or tell me 
what needs to be corrected."

If I request a correction, update the visual summary artifact and ask again 
before moving on.

## Step 4 — Apply the correct recommendation logic

Use the election type determined in Step 1 to decide how to make recommendations:

**If PARTISAN PRIMARY:**
- I am voting only among candidates of one party
- My party is either already known (I stated it in Step 3B) or needs to be 
  determined
- If I selected option 6 in Step 3B: analyze my issue positions from sections 
  C and D and identify which party's platform and candidates best match my stated 
  priorities. Present this analysis as a visual artifact with clear reasoning, 
  then ask me in plain text to confirm which ballot I want before proceeding:
  "Which party ballot would you like to proceed with? Type your choice."
- Within my party's primary, rank candidates by fit to my priorities and recommend 
  the best match in each contested race. For uncontested races, profile the single 
  candidate so I know who I am endorsing.

**If GENERAL ELECTION or NONPARTISAN:**
- Evaluate all candidates across all parties on equal footing
- Recommend the best-fit candidate in each race regardless of party affiliation
- Note party affiliation factually but do not use it as a filter
- Where a race has candidates from multiple parties, compare them directly on 
  issue alignment to my stated priorities

**If RANKED CHOICE / JUNGLE PRIMARY:**
- Rank all candidates by fit to my priorities
- Suggest a full ranking order, not just a top pick
- Explain the strategic implications of ranking order where relevant

**If BALLOT MEASURES:**
- Research the actual text and fiscal/policy impact of each measure
- Map each measure to my stated priorities
- Give a clear Yes or No recommendation with reasoning
- Note who is funding the Yes and No campaigns where relevant

**If MIXED ballot:**
- Apply the appropriate logic to each race or measure individually

## Step 5 — Produce the voter guide

Generate a rich visual artifact as the final voter guide that includes:

- Every race and measure on my ballot
- For each contested race: a visual fit assessment for each candidate mapped to 
  my priorities, a clearly marked recommended pick, and concise reasoning
- For uncontested races: a brief candidate profile card
- The central issue or dynamic defining each race
- A final summary panel of all recommendations in one place, formatted for easy 
  reference when I am standing at the ballot

When rendering alignment tables or fit assessments in the voter guide, never use 
color alone to convey meaning. Always encode alignment levels using at minimum 
two of the following simultaneously:

1. A clear text label: "Strong" / "Moderate" / "Poor" (or "High" / "Medium" / "Low")
2. A distinct symbol or icon that differs in shape, not just color:
   ✓✓ for Strong · ✓ for Moderate · ✗ for Poor
   or: ▰▰▰ filled bars for Strong · ▰▰░ for Moderate · ▰░░ for Poor
3. Color as a supporting layer only — never as the sole differentiator

For candidate fit bar charts, use filled progress bars with a percentage or 
text label alongside, not just bar length or color.

For alignment comparison tables (e.g. Democratic vs Republican ballot by issue), 
use explicit text cells — "Strong" / "Moderate" / "Poor" — rather than symbols 
or dots. If symbols are used, they must differ in shape and include a text label 
in the same cell, e.g. "✓ Strong" or "✗ Poor".

Assume the user may have color vision deficiency. Design all visual output to be 
fully interpretable without relying on color differentiation.

Use candidate cards, fit bar charts, badges, and color coding as a supporting 
layer alongside text labels and shape-distinct symbols to make the guide 
scannable and easy to read. Be factually accurate, politically balanced in 
framing, and transparent about where information is limited or uncertain. 
Present the best case for each candidate fairly. Flag where candidates have 
thin records or where I should do additional research before deciding. 
Let me make the final call.

If I ask follow-up questions about any candidate, race, or ballot measure after 
seeing the guide, research and go deeper on that specific topic, updating the 
visual artifact where appropriate.
